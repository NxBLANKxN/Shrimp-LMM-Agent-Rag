# Challenges and strategies for globally resilient shrimp aquaculture

**Date:** 2026
**Source:** [[s41538-026-00787-7_reference]] (PDF)
**Authors:** Campbell, E., Becker, J.A., Bracher, P. et al.

## Key Findings
- Shrimp aquaculture is a vital component of the global **Blue Foods** system, providing essential protein and micronutrients.
- The sector is dominated by *Litopenaeus vannamei* (Pacific Whiteleg) and *Penaeus monodon* (Black Tiger).
- Four main challenges threaten resilience: **Supply chain vulnerabilities**, **Disease outbreaks** (e.g., WSSV, AHPND, YHV), **Environmental stressors** (Climate change, mangrove loss), and **Economic instability** (debt cycles, certification costs).
- A resilience framework consisting of five strategies is proposed: **Detect** (early warning, diagnostics), **Insulate** (biosecurity, nature-based solutions), **Streamline** (resource efficiency, certification), **Diversify** (alternative feeds, co-cropping), and **Support** (micro-finance, policy).

## Full Summary
This review analyzes the global shrimp aquaculture industry, positioning it as an exemplar of "Blue Foods" production. It details the shift from *Penaeus monodon* to *Litopenaeus vannamei* due to disease outbreaks and the differing characteristics of production systems:
- **Extensive**: Low density, low input, high land/water footprint, often linked to mangrove loss.
- **Semi-intensive**: Moderate density, uses artificial feed and pumping.
- **Intensive**: High density, requires pond lining and aeration, more resource-efficient but economically costly.
- **Super-intensive**: Very high density, often using **Biofloc** or **RAS** (Recirculating Aquaculture Systems), allowing for urban placement and closed-loop production.

### Core Challenges
1. **Supply**: Dependence on commercial feed (fishmeal) and certified disease-free larvae. Vulnerability to geopolitical conflicts (e.g., Russia-Ukraine war) and pandemic-related disruptions.
2. **Disease**: Rapid spread of pathogens like **White Spot Syndrome Virus (WSSV)**, **Acute Hepatopancreatic Necrosis Disease (AHPND)**, and **Yellow Head Virus (YHV)**, leading to catastrophic crop loss.
3. **Environment**: Climate change causes ocean warming, acidification, and extreme weather. The destruction of mangroves for ponds removes natural coastal protection, increasing vulnerability.
4. **Economics**: Smallholders often face a "boom and bust" cycle, high debt to informal intermediaries, and high costs to maintain international certifications (e.g., **BAP**).

### Resilience Strategies
The authors propose a matrix of solutions:
- **Detect**: Using AI, blockchain for traceability, and CRISPR-based rapid diagnostics for early disease detection.
- **Insulate**: Improving biosecurity, adopting solar power, and restoring mangroves to create natural buffers.
- **Streamline**: Improving **Feed Conversion Ratios (FCR)** and adhering to **Good Aquaculture Practices (GAP)** to reduce waste and improve market access.
- **Diversify**: Moving toward alternative protein sources (insects, algae) for feed and implementing polyculture to diversify income.
- **Support**: Implementing micro-finance and state-led debt suspension programs to support small-scale producers.

The paper concludes that meeting the 2050 food demand sustainably requires a shift from purely productivity-focused goals to a holistic, collaborative ecosystem involving all stakeholders in the supply chain.

# Studies on the production enhancement of intensive shrimp pond by partial harvests (高密度養殖蝦池間捕增產之研究)

**Date:** July 2008
**Author:** Shen-Chien Huang (黃尚健)
**Source:** Master Thesis, Institute of Fisheries Science, National Taiwan University

## Key Findings
- **Partial Harvesting Effectiveness**: Implementing partial harvests (harvesting larger shrimp and leaving smaller ones) in high-density ponds significantly increases total production and economic efficiency.
- **Carrying Capacity**: Partial harvesting helps maintain the pond's carrying capacity, reducing biological risks such as oxygen depletion and water quality degradation.
- **Production Increases**:
    - **Kuruma Shrimp (*Penaeus japonicus*)**: Pond A reached 28 tons/ha (18-24g) over 4 months; Pond B reached 10 tons/ha (7-10g) over 3 months.
    - **Grass Shrimp (*Penaeus monodon*)**: In Pond A, partial harvesting increased output by 1.65 tons/ha, with a total output of 7.13 tons/ha over 3 months.
- **Water Quality Dynamics**: In intensive systems, Ammonia-N and Nitrite concentrations increase over time, while pH and Dissolved Oxygen (DO) tend to decrease in the middle and late stages.
- **Aeration**: The use of water wheels is critical for maintaining DO levels and facilitating the nitrification process.

## Full Summary
This thesis investigates the impact of partial harvesting on the production of intensively farmed shrimp. In ultra-high density systems, the high biomass of shrimp, combined with excretion and leftover feed, often leads to severe water quality degradation in the middle and late stages of the farming cycle. This degradation results in slow growth, size disparity, increased disease susceptibility, and in severe cases, mass mortality.

The study introduces the concepts of existing biomass and carrying capacity to manage shrimp ponds. By employing partial harvesting—selectively removing larger individuals that have reached market size—the researcher aimed to simplify management, reduce risks, and increase overall yield.

### Water Quality Analysis
The research monitored several ponds (Kuruma and Grass shrimp) in the Yilan region. Key observations include:
- **Ammonia and Nitrite**: Both showed a clear increasing trend as the farming period progressed.
- **pH and DO**: pH levels generally decreased over time. Dissolved oxygen was highly variable, with significant drops in the bottom layers and during the night, necessitating constant aeration.
- **Plankton**: Early stages were dominated by diatoms (*Nitzschia spp.*, *Cyclotella sp.*) and green algae (*Chlorella sp.*). Late stages shifted toward cyanobacteria (*Oscillatoria spp.*, *Microcystis sp.*), indicating higher organic pollution (eutrophication).

### Partial Harvesting Results
The results demonstrated that partial harvesting allows for higher stocking densities without the typical negative effects on growth and survival.
- For **Kuruma shrimp**, yields were significantly higher than in non-partial harvest ponds.
- For **Grass shrimp**, the strategy not only increased the total tonnage per hectare but also allowed the farmer to sell larger shrimp at higher prices during peak market demand.

### Conclusion
Partial harvesting is concluded to be one of the most effective management strategies for intensive shrimp farming. It maintains a healthier environment, optimizes the use of feed, and ensures a more stable and higher production output compared to traditional single-harvest methods.
